Introduction

Motivation

Fantasy football has evolved from a casual pastime into a billion-dollar industry, with over 50 million people participating in fantasy leagues. One of the most important decisions to be made when drafting is selecting the right quarterback. QBs are often the highest-scoring position in fantasy formats, making their performance prediction crucial for fantasy success. As avid football fans who consistently watch NFL games, track player statistics, and compete in fantasy leagues, we wanted to explore a dataset that reflects our interests while allowing us to draw meaningful, data-driven conclusions.

The quarterback position presents unique analytical challenges for fantasy players. While some believe volume statistics like passing attempts are the key to fantasy success, others argue that draft pedigree or experience level matter more. These debates are often based on intuition or anecdotal evidence rather than rigorous statistical analysis. Our project aims to test these assumptions using real NFL data.

Questions we want to answer

This project seeks to answer these key questions about quarterback fantasy performance:

  1. Does passing volume correlate with fantasy points per game?

    • Is there a significant relationship between pass attempts per game and fantasy points per game?

  2. Does draft position correlate with fantasy points per game?

    • Do early round quarterbacks significantly outperform late round draft picks in fantasy scoring?

  3. Does experience level correlate fantasy points per game?

    • Do veteran QBs (with more career games) perform significantly better than rookies or early-career players?

  4. Can machine learning effectively predict fantasy points per game?

    • Which features are most important?

Why This Matters

Understanding what drives quarterback fantasy performance has several practical applications:

  • Fantasy Draft Strategy: Provides evidence for whether to prioritize high-volume passers, highly-drafted prospects, or experienced veterans
  • Player Valuation: Helps identify whether traditional scouting metrics (draft position) align with actual fantasy production
  • Decision Making: Lets fantasy managers to make data-driven roster decisions instead of relying on hype or their knowledge.

Beyond fantasy football, this analysis shows the complete data science workflow. It starts from data curation and exploratory analysis through hypothesis testing and also machine learning while using a real-world dataset that fans care about. Whether you're optimizing your fantasy lineup or simply curious about what makes quarterbacks successful, this project provides a framework for answering these questions with data.

Data Curation

Our dataset comes from Kaggle: NFL Stats 1999-2022 by Philip Hyde. Specifically, we use the yearly_player_stats_offense.csv file, which contains comprehensive offensive statistics for NFL players across multiple seasons.

This dataset includes player information (ID, name, position, birth year, physical measurements), draft data (draft year, round, pick number, overall selection), season statistics (passing, rushing, and receiving metrics aggregated by season), advanced metrics (passer rating, air yards, red zone performance, and more), and career information (years of experience, games played)

The dataset originally contains 660 columns covering nearly every offensive statistic tracked by the NFL from 2012-2024 which makes it ideal for comprehensive analysis of player performance.

Primary Analysis - Machine Learning Analysis

Problem Setup

Our goal is to predict quarterback fantasy points per game based on their statistical performances. This is clearly a regression problem, as we are predicting a continuous numerical value in fantasy points per game.

Why Linear Regression?

We chose to use linear regression for a variety of reasons. Firstly, with linear regression, it is very easy to interpret our results. We can easily see which stats matter most for fantasy football performance, as linear regression coefficients directly show the relationship between each stat and fantasy points. Intuitively, linear regression makes sense for this problem, as we'd expect that as QBs get more pass attempts, more snaps, or more redzone opportunities, their fantasy points should increase in a relatively proportional way. It is important to note that this does not account for collinearity, but individually each of those assumptions make sense intuitively. Additionally, linear regression is widely used in sports analytics and fantasy football research, making it a good starting point for our analysis.

Why Introduce Lasso Regression?

Throughout the process up until this point, we noticed that multiple of our features (independent variables) are correlated with each other. For example, many of the passing stats are correlated with each other, such as passing attempts per game, passing attempts per game in the redzone, and then passing attempts per game in goal-to-go scenarios. This can cause problems, as we'll see coefficients of variables have odd values (as they will be biased, since the variance in fantasy points per game is better explained by a different independent variable). These biases make it harder to interpret our results, and make it harder to determine which statistics actually matter.

Lasso Regression solves this because it automatically shrinks unimportant features to a coefficient of 0. In addition, it performs feature selection, which means that it identifies the features that are most important to explaining the variance in fantasy points per game. It will make our results much easier to interpret.

Training Approach

We split our training and testing data using a time-based approach: we trained our models on data from 2012-2021, and tested the models on data from 2022-2024. We chose to not randomly split our train and test data because we would essentially be trying to predict past data points while training on future data points.

Conclusion and Key Insights

Visualization: Explaining the Primary Analysis

To clearly show how well our models work, we used two main types of plots. First, we generated Actual vs. Predicted Fantasy Points scatter plots for both the Linear and the Lasso Regression models. In these plots, the goal is to see how close the model's predictions are to the real-world results. The horizontal line on the graph represents the actual fantasy points a quarterback earned in a given season, while the vertical axis shows the predicted score from our model. We also include a red dashed line, which represents what a perfect prediction would look like a 45-degree line where the actual score exactly equals the predicted score. Looking at the plots, the blue dots representing Linear Regression and the green dots representing Lasso Regression are both tightly packed and follow this red dashed line very closely. This tight grouping is visual proof that both models are highly accurate at predicting quarterback fantasy performance. Numerically, this accuracy is confirmed by the high R scores we achieved on our test data: approximately 0.88 for Linear Regression and 0.87 for Lasso Regression. An R value this close to 1.0 means the model is explaining almost 88% of the variability in quarterback fantasy scores.

However, the most important visualization for understanding our main finding is the Lasso Regression Feature Coefficients bar chart. This plot acts like an X-ray, allowing us to see which statistics the model values most when making a prediction. The bars on the chart show the weight or importance assigned to each statistical category or feature. The taller the bar, the more that specific stat influences the final fantasy point prediction. For instance, the stats with the largest positive coefficients are complete_pass_redzone_per_game which is passes completed near the goal line and rush_attempts_per_game which is how often the QB runs the ball. These large coefficients tell us that the most significant drivers of fantasy success are efficiency close to the endzone and a quarterbacks rushing volume. Most importantly, this chart demonstrates the power of the Lasso technique: several bars are missing or have been shrunk to zero (most notably pass_attempts_per_game and snaps_per_game). This is the visual proof that Lasso successfully eliminated unnecessary features.

Insights and Conclusions

Why We Chose Lasso Regression

Our analysis strongly suggests that Lasso Regression is the preferred model for this task compared to the standard Linear Regression. When we trained the models, the Linear Regression used all nine statistical features we initially gave it. In contrast, the Lasso Regression model, thanks to its special regularization technique, automatically identified and eliminated two features (pass_attempts and snaps) by setting their weights to zero. Even though the Lasso model used two fewer pieces of data, its predictive accuracy was nearly identical to the Linear model: Lasso had an R of 0.8728, which is only a minor drop from Linear's R of 0.8858. This finding confirms that the two removed features were largely redundant or useless "noise." Therefore, Lasso is considered the better, more parsimonious model, a scientific term meaning it achieves the same high accuracy with a simpler and cleaner set of predictors. It is more trustworthy because it avoids relying on data that doesn't actually add value to the prediction.

Key Takeaways for the Reader

This project offers critical takeaways for anyone interested in the data science of sports and fantasy football. For a reader new to the topic, the main insight is that not all volume is created equal for a quarterback. Its not just about how often a player is on the field (snaps) or how many times they throw the ball (pass_attempts); the success is driven by high-value plays. Our Lasso model explicitly filtered out those generic volume metrics in favor of more efficient and impactful statistics like red zone pass completions and rushing attempts. This means that the biggest gains in fantasy scoring come from converting opportunities near the goal line and using their legs to gain yards and score.

For an informed reader or fantasy expert who already understands the sport, this project provides data-driven evidence to refine their strategy. While conventional wisdom often stresses that "volume is king," our analysis adds a necessary quality vs. quantity distinction. The model's selection process proves that statistics related to generic play frequency are highly correlated with (and thus redundant to) the true drivers of fantasy points. Essentially, you should prioritize a quarterback who is an efficient rusher and is trusted to throw the ball in the most critical area of the field (the red zone) over a quarterback who simply attempts a high number of passes between the 20-yard lines. Our results show that the features that truly determine fantasy point totals are those that lead directly to touchdowns and first downs.
